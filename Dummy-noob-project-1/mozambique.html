Political Views

Mozambique's political landscape is currently quite turbulent. The country recently experienced significant unrest following the presidential and parliamentary elections in October 2024. The ruling party, Frelimo, claimed a landslide victory, but these results have been widely contested, leading to sustained and widespread protests2. The opposition, led by Venâncio Mondlane, has called for new demonstrations, citing widespread voter fraud

The unrest reflects broader discontent with the socioeconomic status quo, including limited social mobility and growing inequalities. The Southern African Development Community (SADC) has called for an extraordinary summit to address the political crisis

Mozambique's political system is a semi-presidential representative democratic republic, with the President serving as both head of state and head of government. The country has a history of political instability, including a long civil war and ongoing challenges with corruption and economic mismanagement

Culture

People and Ethnic Groups
Main Ethnic Groups: The country is home to various ethnic groups, including the Makhuwa, Tsonga, Makonde, Shangaan, Shona, Sena, Ndau, and other indigenous groups.

Languages: Portuguese is the official language, but many local languages are spoken, such as Emakhuwa, Xichangana, and Cisena.

Religion
Religious Practices: The majority of Mozambicans practice Christianity (mainly Roman Catholicism), but there are also significant Muslim, Indigenous African, and other religious communities.
Arts and Music
Musical Instruments: Traditional instruments include handmade drums, the lupembe (a woodwind instrument), and the marimba (a type of xylophone).

Music and Dance: Music is integral to religious ceremonies and traditional celebrations, with each ethnic group having its own unique musical styles and dances
Cuisine
Staple Foods: Cassava (manioc) and maize (cornmeal) are staple foods, often served with sauces made from cassava leaves, peanuts, or coconut.

Popular Dishes: Matapa, a dish made from cassava leaves cooked with ground peanuts or coconut milk, is a common and beloved dish.
Social Structure
Family and Community: Mozambican society traditionally revolves around the family and village, with customs and observances rooted in local rather than national influences.

Traditional Values: Despite modern influences, many traditional values and practices, such as herbal healing and rites of passage, remain integral to Mozambican culture.
History and Independence
Independence: Mozambique gained independence from Portugal in 1975, allowing its culture to develop freely since then.

Cultural Heritage: The country's cultural heritage is a blend of its diverse historical influences, creating a unique and vibrant cultural identity.

Economic Status

Economic Overview
GDP: Mozambique's GDP was approximately $14.396 billion (nominal) in 2018.

Growth Rate: The economy is expected to grow by around 4% per year from 2024 to 2026.

Main Sectors: Agriculture (29.5%), industry (23.9%), and services (46.5%).

Natural Resources
Energy: Mozambique has significant natural gas reserves, especially in the northern province of Cabo Delgado.

Minerals: The country is rich in minerals like coal, aluminum, and natural gas.

Agriculture: Subsistence agriculture employs the majority of the workforce, with crops like cassava, maize, and cashews being important.
Trade
Exports: Major export goods include aluminum, coal, bulk electricity, lumber, cotton, prawns, cashews, sugar, and citrus.

Main Export Partners: European Union, India, South Africa, South Korea, and China.

Imports: Key imports are fuel, chemicals, machinery, vehicles, metal products, textiles, and food.

Main Import Partners: South Africa, South Korea, China, India, and the European Union.
Challenges
Poverty: About 52% of the population lives below the poverty line.

Conflict: The ongoing conflict in Cabo Delgado has disrupted economic activities and stalled progress in key LNG investments.

Dependence on Foreign Aid: Despite economic growth, Mozambique still relies on foreign assistance for a significant portion of its budget.
Future Prospects
Economic Recovery: The economic recovery is gaining momentum, with growth driven by the extractive sector and the service sector.

Strategic Location: Mozambique's location along the Indian Ocean and its borders with several landlocked countries make it a gateway to global markets.

Languages

Official Language
Portuguese: The official language of Mozambique, used in government, education, and media. It is a legacy of Mozambique's colonial past.

Indigenous Languages Mozambique has several indigenous languages spoken by different ethnic groups. Some of the most widely spoken include:

Emakhuwa: The most spoken indigenous language, primarily used in the northern regions.

Xichangana (Tsonga): Spoken in the southern part of the country.

Cisena (Sena): Commonly spoken in the central regions.

Elomwe: Used in the central and northern areas.

Chuwabo: Found mainly in the Zambezia Province.

Echuwabo: Spoken by communities in the northern parts of the country.

Shimakonde: Used by the Makonde people in the northern regions.
Cultural Importance
Multilingual Society: Many Mozambicans are multilingual, often speaking Portuguese alongside one or more indigenous languages.

Preservation Efforts: There are ongoing efforts to preserve and promote the use of indigenous languages through education and cultural programs.
Communication and Daily Life
Urban vs Rural: In urban areas, Portuguese is more commonly spoken, while in rural areas, indigenous languages are more prevalent.

Education: While Portuguese is the medium of instruction in schools, there are initiatives to incorporate indigenous languages into the education system.

Ethnic Groups

1. Makua
Location: Predominantly in the northern provinces.

Population: The largest ethnic group in Mozambique.

Culture: Known for their traditional crafts, agriculture, and vibrant musical traditions.

2. Tsonga (Shangaan) Location: Primarily in the southern regions of Mozambique.

Population: Significant presence, especially around the Gaza and Maputo provinces.

Culture: They are known for their dance, music, and rich traditions in storytelling.
3. Makonde
Location: Found in the northern part of the country, especially in the Cabo Delgado province.

Culture: Renowned for their intricate wood carvings and masks, which are highly valued in Mozambique and beyond.
4. Sena
Location: Predominantly in the central regions, particularly along the Zambezi River.

Culture: Traditionally involved in agriculture, they also have a rich cultural heritage with unique dance and music forms.
5. Shona
Location: Found mostly in the central provinces, close to the Zimbabwean border.

Culture: Known for their historical significance and traditional practices in both agriculture and artisanal crafts.
6. Swahili
Location: Mainly along the northern coastal areas.

Culture: They play a significant role in trade and are known for their coastal maritime culture.
7. Yao
Location: Found in the northern and north-central regions.

Culture: Known for their involvement in trade and their traditional leadership structures.
8. Lomwe
Location: Central and northern regions, with significant populations in Zambezia and Nampula provinces.

Culture: Their cultural practices include unique ceremonies and a strong agricultural tradition.
9. Ronga
Location: Primarily in the southern regions near Maputo.

Culture: Closely related to the Tsonga, with similar cultural practices and language.

GDP Performance

Recent GDP Performance
2023: Mozambique's GDP grew by an estimated 5.0%. This growth was driven mainly by the extractive industries, particularly liquefied natural gas processing in the Coral South Field2.

2022: The GDP growth rate was 4.2%, showing a steady recovery from the economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

2021: The economy expanded by 2.5%, marking a return to positive growth after a contraction in 2020.

2020: The GDP shrank by 1.2%, the first decline in recent years due to the pandemic.

Economic Outlook
2024-2025: Real GDP is projected to grow by an average of 5.2% per year, driven by the extractives sector, particularly gas production.

Inflation: Inflation is expected to fall to an average of 4.8% between 2024 and 2025 due to prudent monetary policy.

Current Account Deficit: The current account deficit is projected to increase to 38.1% of GDP in 2024 and 43.0% in 2025 as imports rise.
Challenges
Poverty: Despite economic growth, poverty remains high at an estimated 74.5% in 2023.

Debt Distress: Mozambique is in debt distress, but its debt is assessed as sustainable on a forward-looking basis.
