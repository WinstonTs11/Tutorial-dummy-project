Political Views

Monarchy
King Mswati III: He has been the king since 1986 and holds supreme executive, legislative, and judicial powers. The king is also known as the Ngwenyama (Lion).

Ndlovukati:> The queen mother or a female relative who serves as a spiritual and national figurehead alongside the king.

Government Structure
Tinkhundla System: Eswatini's political system is based on the Tinkhundla (constituencies) system, which emphasizes devolution of power from the central government to local communities.

Parliament: The bicameral parliament consists of the Senate and the House of Assembly. Members are either elected through the Tinkhundla system or appointed by the king.
Political Participation
Limited Political Parties: Political parties are not officially recognized and cannot contest elections. The political landscape is dominated by individual merit and traditional structures.

Human Rights Issues: There are significant concerns about human rights, including restrictions on political participation, media freedom, and reports of police brutality during political demonstrations.
Recent Developments

Pro-Democracy Protests: There have been increasing calls for democratic reforms and national dialogue, especially following intense pro-democracy protests in recent years.

Culture

Traditional Ceremonies
Incwala (First Fruits Ceremony): This is the most significant cultural event in Eswatini, held annually around December. It involves rituals to thank the gods for the harvest and to bless the king.

Umhlanga (Reed Dance): An annual event where thousands of young maidens gather to celebrate their purity and present reeds to the queen mother. It’s a colorful and vibrant festival showcasing Swazi customs.

Music and Dance
Sibhaca: A traditional dance performed by men, characterized by high-energy stomping and vibrant costumes.

Indlamu: Another traditional dance, often performed during celebrations and ceremonies, involving vigorous movements and rhythmic drumming.

Music: Eswatini’s music includes traditional African rhythms and instruments, with modern influences blending seamlessly into traditional melodies.
Art and Craft
Handicrafts: The country is known for its high-quality handicrafts, including woven baskets, beadwork, and pottery. The Swazi Candles, made in unique and colorful designs, are a popular artisan product.

Textiles: Traditional attire, such as the brightly colored emahiya wraps, is an important part of Swazi identity and often seen during cultural events.
Cuisine
Staple Foods: The Swazi diet mainly consists of maize (corn), sorghum, and goat meat, with vegetables and legumes also forming part of daily meals.

Traditional Dishes: One popular dish is “sishwala,” a thick porridge made from maize meal, often served with meat or vegetables.
Social Structure
Community and Family: Eswatini society is deeply communal, with extended families living together and community life playing a central role.

Chiefdoms: The country is divided into various chiefdoms, each led by a chief who plays a crucial role in governance and the maintenance of cultural traditions.
Language
Siswati: The national language, spoken by nearly all Swazis, used in daily communication, media, and education.

English: Also an official language, used in government, business, and education.

Economic Status

Economic Overview
GDP Growth: Eswatini's economy grew from 0.5% in 2022 to an estimated 4.8% in 2023, driven by a stronger performance in the services sector.

Per Capita GDP: The GDP per capita was approximately $3,823 in 2023.

Main Sectors: The economy is slightly more than half services, with industry (particularly manufacturing) comprising another third.

Trade and Partners
Main Trading Partner: South Africa is Eswatini's main trading partner, accounting for about 65% of its exports and 75% of its imports.

Trade Balance: The trade balance improved, contributing to a current account surplus of 3.9% of GDP in 2023.
Fiscal Situation
Fiscal Deficit: The fiscal deficit declined to 2.0% of GDP in 2023 from 6.5% in 2022, thanks to high revenues from the Southern African Customs Union (SACU).

Public Debt: Public debt declined to below 40% of GDP.
Challenges
Unemployment: Unemployment remains high, with a rate of 33.3% in 2021, the highest on record in over a decade.

Poverty: Over 50% of the population lives below the $36.5/day poverty line.

Inequality: Eswatini has high and persistent inequality, with a Gini index of 54.6% in 2016.
Social Indicators
Life Expectancy: Life expectancy at birth is 57 years as of 2021.

HIV/AIDS Prevalence: Eswatini has one of the highest HIV/AIDS prevalence rates in the world, although recent years have shown declines in new infections.

Languages

Siswati
National Language: Siswati (also known as Swazi) is the national language and is spoken by the majority of the population.

Usage: It is used in everyday communication, education, and media.

Cultural Importance: Siswati plays a crucial role in preserving Swazi culture and traditions.

English
Official Language: English is the second official language of Eswatini.

Usage: It is used in government, business, and education, and serves as a lingua franca for communication between different ethnic groups.
Other Languages Tsonga and Zulu: Other languages, such as Tsonga and Zulu, are also spoken by smaller communities within Eswatini.

Multilingualism: Many Swazis are multilingual, often speaking Siswati, English, and another regional language.

Ethnic Groups

1. Swazi Population: The majority of the population is ethnic Swazi.

Culture: The Swazi people have a rich cultural heritage, with traditional ceremonies, music, and dance playing a significant role in their society.

2. Zulu Population: There is a small but significant population of Zulu people.

Culture: The Zulu people share cultural similarities with the Swazi, including language and traditional practices.
3. Sotho
Population: The Sotho people also form a part of the ethnic composition.
Culture: They have their own distinct cultural practices and traditions.
4. Tsonga Population: The Tsonga people are another ethnic group in Eswatini.

Culture: Known for their unique cultural practices, including traditional dances and crafts.
5. Europeans
Population: There is a small population of Europeans, predominantly of British and Afrikaner origin.

Culture: They contribute to the multicultural fabric of Eswatini, bringing their own cultural influences.
6. Mixed Ancestry
Population: There is also a segment of the population with mixed ancestry, combining elements from various ethnic backgrounds.

GDP Performance

Recent GDP Performance
2023: Eswatini's GDP grew by an estimated 4.8%. This growth was driven mainly by a stronger performance in the services sector1.

2022: The GDP growth rate was 3.91%, representing a change of 183 million USD over 2021.

2021: The economy expanded by 2.5%, marking a return to positive growth after a contraction in 2020.

2020: The GDP shrank by 1.2%, the first decline in recent years due to the pandemic.

Economic Outlook
2024-2025: Real GDP is projected to grow by an average of 4.6% per year, driven by both domestic and foreign demand.

Inflation: Inflationary pressures eased in the first eight months of 2024, with annual inflation averaging 4.2% during this period.

Fiscal Deficit: The fiscal deficit is estimated to have declined to 2.0% of GDP in 2023 from 6.5% in 2022, thanks to high revenues from the Southern African Customs Union (SACU).

Public Debt: Public debt declined to below 40% of GDP.
Challenges
Unemployment: Unemployment remains high, with a rate of 33.3% in 2021, the highest on record in over a decade.

Poverty: Over 50% of the population lives below the $3.65/day poverty line.

Inequality: Eswatini has high and persistent inequality, with a Gini index of 54.6% in 2016.
