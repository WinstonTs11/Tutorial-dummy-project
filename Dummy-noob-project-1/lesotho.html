Political Views

Government Structure
Monarchy: King Letsie III serves as the ceremonial head of state. The king's role is largely symbolic, with no executive powers.

Prime Minister: The head of government is the Prime Minister, currently Sam Matekane, who took office on October 28, 20222. The Prime Minister is the leader of the majority party in the National Assembly.

Parliament: Lesotho's Parliament is bicameral, consisting of the Senate and the National Assembly. The National Assembly has 120 members, elected for a five-year term, with 80 elected in single-seat constituencies and 40 by proportional representation1.

Political Parties
Revolution for Prosperity (RFP): A newly established party that won the 2022 general elections, led by businessman Sam Matekane3.

Democratic Congress (DC): One of the major political parties, which has been a significant player in Lesotho's politics.

All Basotho Convention (ABC): Previously a dominant party, it lost significant ground in the 2022 elections.
Recent Developments
2022 Elections: The 2022 general elections were notable for the emergence of the RFP, which won 56 of the 120 seats in the National Assembly. The elections were considered peaceful and fair by international observers3.

Coalition Government: The RFP formed a coalition government with the Alliance of Democrats (AD) and the Movement for Economic Change (MEC).
Challenges
Political Instability: Lesotho has faced political instability, with frequent changes in government and coalition dynamics.

Socio-Economic Issues: The country grapples with high poverty rates, unemployment, and inequality.

Culture

Traditional Ceremonies and Practices
Initiation Rites: Boys and girls undergo initiation ceremonies called "lebollo" and "bojale," marking their transition to adulthood. These rites are significant cultural events involving traditional education and rituals.

Chieftaincy: Traditional leadership is an integral part of Basotho culture, with chiefs playing crucial roles in governance and community matters.

Music and Dance
Music: Traditional music includes instruments like the "lekope" (a type of bow) and the "moropa" (drum). Modern genres like Famo, which combines accordion music with vocals, are also popular.

Dance: Traditional dances such as "mokhibo" (performed by women) and "mohobelo" (performed by men) are important at celebrations and ceremonies.
Clothing
Basotho Blanket: The iconic Basotho blanket is a key element of cultural identity, worn during ceremonies and daily life. These blankets are often brightly colored and patterned, serving both practical and symbolic purposes.

Traditional Attire: Men wear "deka" (traditional wraps) and women wear "seshoeshoe" (colorful dresses), especially during cultural events.
Art and Craft
Weaving: Lesotho is known for its high-quality weaving, particularly the production of woven baskets and mats.

Pottery and Beadwork: Traditional crafts include pottery and intricate beadwork, often used in jewelry and decorative items.
Cuisine
Staple Foods: The Basotho diet includes staples like maize, sorghum, and wheat. Common dishes are "papa" (a maize porridge) and "moroho" (wild spinach).

Traditional Dishes: Dishes like "motoho" (fermented sorghum porridge) and "korokoro" (a type of bread) are traditional favorites.
Social Structure and Community Life
Family and Community: Lesotho is a communal society with strong family ties and a focus on mutual aid within communities.

Village Life: Traditional village life involves cooperative farming and communal activities, reinforcing social bonds.
Language
Sesotho: The primary language spoken is Sesotho, used in daily communication, education, and media. English is also an official language, used in government and business.

Economic Status

Economic Overview
GDP: Lesotho's GDP was approximately $2.73 billion USD in 2019. The GDP per capita is around $878 USD as of 2023.

Growth Rate: The economy expanded by 0.9% in 2023, driven mainly by the public sector and construction activities. The GDP growth rate is projected to average 2.4% over 2024-2025.

Main Sectors
Agriculture: About 80% of the population is engaged in subsistence agriculture.

Manufacturing: The textile and apparel industry is a significant part of the economy, with garments accounting for a large portion of exports.

Mining: The mining sector, particularly diamonds, plays a crucial role in the economy.

Tourism: Tourism is an emerging sector, with the country's natural beauty attracting visitors.

Remittances: Remittances from Basotho working in South Africa are a vital source of income for many families.
Trade and Partners
Exports: Major export goods include garments (53%) and diamonds (47%).

Main Export Partners: The United States (60%), Europe (17%), and the Southern African Customs Union (SACU) (19%).

Imports: Lesotho imports food, building materials, vehicles, machinery, medicines, and petroleum products.

Main Import Partners: SACU countries, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and China.
Challenges Unemployment: The unemployment rate is high, with estimates ranging from 24% to 28%.

Poverty: Over 50% of the population lives below the poverty line.

Inflation: Inflation has been a challenge, with the headline rate declining to 6.7% in July 2024 from a peak of 9.8% in July 2022.
Economic Outlook
Public Sector and Construction: The Lesotho Highlands Water Project-II (LHWP-II) is a significant driver of economic growth, impacting transportation, logistics, and financial services.

Fiscal Position: The fiscal deficit is expected to turn into a surplus in the medium term, supported by higher SACU revenue and modest growth in domestic tax revenue.

Current Account Deficit: The current account deficit improved from 8.4% of GDP in 2022 to an estimated 3.4% in 2023.

Languages

Primary Languages
Sesotho

Status: Sesotho is the national and official language of Lesotho.

Usage: It is spoken by nearly all Basotho people and is used in daily communication, education, and media.

Features: Sesotho is a Bantu language known for its rich oral tradition and poetic forms of expression.
English

Status: English is also an official language.

Usage: It is used in government, business, and formal education. English serves as a lingua franca for communication with non-Sesotho speakers and in international contexts.

Other Languages
Zulu and Xhosa

Usage: These languages are spoken by smaller communities within Lesotho, particularly among people of Zulu and Xhosa descent.
Language in Education and Media
Education: Both Sesotho and English are used as mediums of instruction in schools, with English becoming more predominant at higher educational levels.

Media: Local media, including newspapers, radio, and television, often use both languages to cater to a broad audience.

Ethnic Groups

1. Basotho
Population: The vast majority of Lesotho's population consists of the Basotho people.

Language: The Basotho primarily speak Sesotho, the national language of Lesotho.

Culture: The Basotho culture is rich with traditional practices, music, dance, and a strong sense of community and family. Important cultural symbols include the Basotho blanket and the mokorotlo (a traditional hat).

Other Ethnic Groups
While Lesotho is predominantly Basotho, there are small populations of other ethnic groups:

Nguni: Some individuals of Zulu and Xhosa descent live in Lesotho, often as part of migrant communities.

Europeans: There is a minor population of European descent, primarily of British and Afrikaner heritage.

GDP Performance

Recent GDP Performance
2023: The economy expanded by 0.9%. This growth was driven mainly by the public sector and construction activities3.

2022: The GDP growth rate was 1.29%, a slight decline from 1.85% in 2021.

2021: The economy grew by 1.85%, marking a recovery from the -7.46% contraction in 2020.

2020: The GDP shrank by 7.46%, largely due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Economic Outlook
2024-2025: The domestic economy is projected to grow by 2.4% on average over this period. Construction activity and associated knock-on effects on the service industry are expected to continue shaping growth3.

Inflation: Inflation is projected to average 6.2% in 2023, gradually moderating to 4.9% in 2025.

Fiscal Position: The fiscal deficit is expected to turn into a surplus in the medium term, supported by higher SACU revenue and modest growth in domestic tax revenue.
